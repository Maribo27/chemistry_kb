Amino Acids — Organic Compounds molecule which simultaneously contains carboxyl and amine groups. The basic chemical elements of amino acids are carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), and nitrogen (N), although other elements are also found in the radical of certain amino acids. About 500 naturally occurring amino acids are known (although only 20 are used in the genetic code). Amino acids can be considered as derivatives of carboxylic acids in which one or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by amino groups.

Depending on the radical, amino acids are subdivided into:
  • Non-Polar: glycine, alanine, in valine, isoleucine, leucine, proline
  • Polar uncharged (charge compensated) at pH = 7: serine, threonine, cysteine ​​, methionine, asparagine, glutamine
  • Aromatics: phenylalanine, tryptofan, tyrosine
  • Polar charged negatively at pH = 7: aspartate, glutamic_acid
  • Polar charged positively at pH = 7: lysine, arginine, histidine

According to the functional group, they can be divided into:
  • Aliphatic - compounds that do not contain aromatic bonds.
  • Aromatic - compounds containing aromatic bonds.
  • Heterocyclic - organic compounds containing cycles that also contain atoms of other elements along with carbon.
  • Imino acids - are carboxylic acids containing an imino group (NH).

Also, amino acids are divided into two large groups where possible organisms synthesize them from their predecessors
  • Essential
    • For most animals and humans, essential amino acids are: valine, isoleucine, leucine, threonine, methionine, lysine, >phenylalanine, tryptofan.
  • Replaceable
    • For most animals and humans, the essential amino acids are: glycine, alanine, proline, serine, cysteine ​​, aspartate, asparagine, glutamic acid, glutamine, tyrosine.

Physical properties and structure of amino acids

The physical properties of amino acids differ sharply from the corresponding acids and bases. All of them are crystalline substances, dissolve better in water than in organic solvents, have rather high melting points; many of them have a sweet taste. These properties clearly indicate the salt-like character of these compounds. Features of the physical and chemical properties of amino acids are due to their structure - the presence of two opposite functional properties of the groups: acid and basic.

Chemical properties

All amino acids are amphoteric compounds, they can exhibit both acidic properties due to the presence of the carboxyl group —COOH in their molecules, and basic properties due to the amino group —NH2. Amino acids interact with acids and alkalis :
NH2—CH2—COOH + HCl → HCl • NH2—CH2—COOH (hydrochloric salt glycine)
NH2—CH2—COOH + NaOH → H2O + NH2—CH2—COONa (sodium salt glycine)
Due to this, amino acid solutions in water have the properties of buffer solutions, that is, they are in a state of internal salts.
NH2—CH2COOH ↔ N+H3—CH2COO-
Amino acids can usually enter into all reactions characteristic of carboxylic acids and amines .
Esterification :
NH2—CH2—COOH + CH3 OH → H2O + NH2—CH2—COOCH3(glycine methyl ester)

An important feature of amino acids is their ability to polycondensation, leading to the formation of polyamides, including peptides , proteins , nylon, capron.
Reaction of formation of peptides :
HOOC—CH2—NH—H + HOOC—CH2—NH2 → HOOC—CH2- NH—CO—CH2—NH2 + H2O

Amino Acquisition

Most of the amino acids can be obtained by hydrolysis proteins or as a result of chemical reactions:
CH3COOH + Cl2 + (catalyst) → CH2ClCOOH + HCl
CH2ClCOOH + 2NH3 → NH2—CH2COOH + NH4Cl

Amino Acquisition

Amino acids are widely used in modern pharmacology. Being not only structural elements of proteins and other endogenous compounds, they have a great functional significance. Some of them act as neurotransmitter substances. Some amino acids have found independent use as medicines.
